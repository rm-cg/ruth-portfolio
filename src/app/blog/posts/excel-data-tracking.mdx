## The Reality of Messy Organizational Data

As the Lead Secretariat for GDGoC - PLM and a Deputy Secretary for the PLM Mathematical Society, I handle a massive influx of data on a weekly basis. Whether it is tracking the 2,874 ticket sales from our fundraising campaign or processing post-event feedback forms from hundreds of attendees, raw data is rarely ever clean. 

Names are typed in lowercase, email addresses have trailing spaces, and duplicate registrations are inevitable. For a long time, I relied on complex nested formulas to clean this data. But as my datasets grew, these formulas made the workbooks incredibly slow and prone to breaking. That is when I shifted my workflow to **Power Query**.

## Enter Power Query: The Built-in ETL Tool

Many people treat Excel simply as a digital ledger, but underneath its surface lies Power Query—a robust **Extract, Transform, Load (ETL)** engine. 

Instead of writing temporary formulas in adjacent columns, Power Query allows you to connect to a raw data source, apply a sequence of cleaning steps, and load a perfectly structured dataset into a Data Model or Pivot Table. The best part? It records every single step. When new event data comes in next month, I simply hit "Refresh," and the entire cleaning process happens automatically.

## Automating the Cleanup with M-Code

While Power Query has a fantastic user interface, its true power lies in its underlying functional language: **M-Code**. 

When cross-referencing attendance sheets with our master registration database, I use M-Code to standardize text formats and filter out invalid entries before any analysis even begins. Here is a snippet of the backend code I use to automate our attendee profiling:

<CodeBlock
    marginBottom="16"
    highlight="3-4"
    codes={[
      {
        code:
`let
    Source = Excel.CurrentWorkbook(){[Name="Raw_Registrants"]}[Content],
    CleanedText = Table.TransformColumns(Source,{{"Full_Name", Text.Proper, type text}}),
    FilteredNulls = Table.SelectRows(CleanedText, each [Email_Address] <> null),
    RemovedDuplicates = Table.Distinct(FilteredNulls, {"Email_Address"})
in
    RemovedDuplicates`,
        language: "powerquery",
        label: "attendee_preprocessing.pq"
      }
    ]}
/>

### Breaking Down the Logic:
1. **`Text.Proper`**: Automatically scans the `Full_Name` column and converts all text to Title Case (e.g., transforming "juan dela cruz" to "Juan Dela Cruz"), ensuring our certificate generation is perfectly formatted.
2. **`FilteredNulls`**: Instantly removes any row where the `Email_Address` is missing, preventing bounce-backs during our email campaigns.
3. **`Table.Distinct`**: Scans the dataset and removes duplicate submissions based on the email address, ensuring our attendance metrics are entirely accurate.
