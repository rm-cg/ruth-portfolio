## The Math Journey

As a BS Mathematics student, you quickly realize that standard word processors are incredibly inefficient for writing long proofs and complex formulas. You often spend more time fighting with the built-in equation editor and fixing broken formatting than actually solving the math problem. 

Transitioning to LaTeX changed everything for me. It allows you to create, edit, and format mathematical documents with absolute precision simply by writing code. I now use it extensively not just for formal research papers, but for compiling my daily math homeworks and creating clean, readable reviewers for my exams. 

## Document Setup

The power of LaTeX starts in the preamble. Instead of manually adjusting margins and fonts, the preamble is used to set the document's metadata and formatting rules globally. It is used to load specialized packages for math symbols, graphics, and data tables.

<CodeBlock
    marginBottom="16"
    codes={[
      {
        code:
`\\documentclass[12pt, a4paper]{article}
\\usepackage{amsmath, amssymb}
\\usepackage{graphicx}

\\title{Quantitative Research Findings}
\\author{Ruth Margareth Guinto}
\\date{\\today}

\\begin{document}
\\maketitle`,
        language: "latex",
        label: "research_paper.tex"
      }
    ]}
/>

## The Beauty of Mathematical Syntax

The absolute biggest benefit of LaTeX is how incredibly clean the math formulas look, no matter how ridiculously complicated they get. By using LaTeX syntax, elements like nested fractions, integrals, limits, and massive matrices are automatically transformed into professional, textbook-quality typography. 

You do not have to worry about the spacing getting ruined when you add a new line or a complex symbol; the compiler handles the mathematical design perfectly so you can just focus on the logic. 

## Complex Environments

You can use custom environments in `.tex` files to format high-level statistical tests. For example, compiling my reviewer for my Statistics subject, writing out complex formulas like the Two-Sample Z-Test and Confidence Intervals was simple using the `equation` environment.

<CodeBlock
    marginBottom="16"
    highlight="2, 6"
    codes={[
      {
        code:
`\\begin{equation}
    Z_{comp} = \\frac{(\\bar{x}_1 - \\bar{x}_2) - (\\mu_1 - \\mu_2)}{\\sqrt{\\frac{\\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \\frac{\\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}}
\\end{equation}

\\begin{equation}
    C.I. = \\hat{p} \\pm Z_{critical} \\sqrt{\\frac{\\hat{p}(1-\\hat{p})}{n}}
\\end{equation}`,
        language: "latex",
        label: "statistics_reviewer.tex"
      }
    ]}
/>

As you can see, the `equation` environment makes handling messy structures like nested fractions and square roots natively available for use when you import the `amsmath` package. You can add more complex mathematical logic by simply passing different variables to the structured objects without worrying about breaking the formatting.

## Professional Output & Collaboration

While formatting heavily relies on code, the final compilation of a LaTeX document results in a flawless, structured PDF that is highly respected in the fields of Data Science, Mathematics, and academic research.
